# Oficio Taller — Audience Segments

**For:** Lucía (audience intelligence), Marco (creative brief), Carmen (learning loop)
**Updated:** March 2026 — baseline. Update after each quarterly Pulso review.

---

## How to use this document

These segments are not demographic buckets. They are portraits of the kinds of people who genuinely align with what Oficio Taller does — and the kinds who don't. Lucía uses them to match projects to audiences. Carmen updates them when Pulso data reveals drift or new patterns.

A person can belong to more than one segment. A project can speak to more than one. What matters is: which segment does *this specific project* speak to most directly, and what language does that segment use?

---

## Segment 1 — El que Habita / The Place-Dweller

**Who they are:**
People who choose where they live based on landscape, light, and the quality of daily life in a place. They may have lived abroad or in cities and made a conscious choice to build or buy somewhere specific. They are not building a second home as an investment — they are building a place they intend to inhabit deeply.

**What they value:**
- The bond between the built space and the land it sits on
- The feeling that a house belongs to its place, not imposed on it
- Daily rituals: the morning light, the sound of wind or water, the temperature of a material underfoot
- Craft and permanence over trend and renovation cycles
- Privacy, stillness, the absence of spectacle

**Language they use naturally:**
- "I want it to feel like it grew there"
- "The way the light comes in at 6am"
- "I don't want it to look like every other house in the area"
- "I want to wake up and feel like I'm still outside"
- "Something that has a relationship with the landscape"
- "Not a vacation home — a place we actually live"
- "Materials that age well, that tell the story of the place"

**Where they appear:**
- Long YouTube watch times (full film, not just first 30 seconds)
- Instagram saves (not likes — saves, meaning they're returning to it)
- Direct inquiry emails that describe their site before describing their brief
- Referrals from past clients who share the same values

**Green flags in an inquiry:**
- Describes the land or site in detail before describing the house
- Mentions existing vegetation, views, or seasonal conditions
- Has been watching or following the studio for months before reaching out
- Asks about the studio's process, not their portfolio

**Red flags:**
- Asks about square footage in the first message
- Sends a Pinterest board of 40 unrelated reference images
- Describes the project primarily in terms of resale value

## Segment 2 — El Constructor Reflexivo / The Thoughtful Builder

**Who they are:**
People who have built or renovated before and found the experience either disappointing or transformative. They know what they don't want. They have taste but are not designers — they need a studio that can translate their instincts into something real. They may have more budget than Segment 1 but are not motivated by status display.

**What they value:**
- A studio that listens before proposing
- Transparency about process, decisions, and trade-offs
- Materials that are honest about what they are
- A result that feels personal, not generic
- The sense that the studio understood something about them and their life

**Language they use:**
- "We've seen too many houses that look like they could be anywhere"
- "We want something that feels Mexican but not kitschy"
- "We don't need it to be impressive — we need it to feel right"
- "How do you approach a project like this?"
- "We've worked with architects before and it wasn't what we expected"

**Where they appear:**
- Website visits with long session times on individual project pages
- Instagram DMs with a specific question about a project or material
- Referrals from contractors or other architects who know the studio's work
- Reach out after seeing a specific project that resonates

**Green flags:**
- References a specific project of the studio's and says why it resonated
- Has a clear sense of how they want to live, even if not how they want the house to look
- Asks about the studio's relationship with local craftspeople or builders

**Red flags:**
- Compares the studio to a competitor primarily on price
- Expects 3D renders before a site visit
- Treats the project as primarily a financial decision

## Segment 3 — El Aficionado al Oficio / The Craft Admirer

**Who they are:**
Architects, interior designers, students, craftspeople, and serious design enthusiasts who follow the studio for the quality of the work itself. They may never commission a project but they amplify the studio's reputation — sharing content, referencing the work in conversations, sending clients their way.

**What they value:**
- Materiality and craft detail
- The thinking behind decisions (why that material, why that joint, why that threshold)
- Process imagery — not just the finished result
- Honesty about difficulty, failure, and iteration
- Regional specificity — architecture that could only exist in that place

**Language they use:**
- Comments on specific details: "the way that concrete meets the wood frame here"
- Asks about process: "how did you handle the transition between inside and outside?"
- Shares posts with a specific note: "this is exactly what I mean by belonging to a place"
- Follows for years before engaging publicly

**Where they appear:**
- YouTube comments with technical or appreciative specificity
- Instagram saves and reshares to Stories
- Architecture forums, schools, press
- Recommendations to clients who ask for a referral

**Value to the studio:**
Not primary lead source but critical for reputation and referral. Content that resonates with this segment elevates the studio's standing in the professional community.

## Misaligned Audiences — Suppress Content That Attracts These

### The Luxury Tourist
Attracted by: beautiful imagery, exotic locations, aspirational lifestyle content
Signal: generic comments ("stunning", "goals", "dream home"), follows many luxury real estate accounts
Problem: will never commission a project; inflates metrics without value; attracts more of the same

### The Flipper / Developer
Attracted by: polished finished product, famous location, perceived investment value
Signal: asks about cost per square meter, turnaround time, resale comps
Problem: fundamentally misaligned with the studio's process and values

### The Instagram Architect
Attracted by: high-contrast photography, dramatic angles, render-quality imagery
Signal: engages with image quality not spatial quality; follows for aesthetics not inhabitation
Problem: generates engagement that misleads the Iteration Pod into thinking content is working

## Signals Lucía Uses to Score Engagement Quality

| Signal | Weight | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| YouTube full watch (>80%) | Very high | Deep engagement with the project's narrative |
| Instagram save | High | Returning to it — resonance, not reflex |
| Specific comment referencing detail | High | Segment 1, 2, or 3 — genuine attention |
| Direct inquiry email describing site | Very high | Segment 1 — highest fit |
| Generic positive comment | Low | May be misaligned audience |
| Follower gain spike | Very low | Quantity signal, not quality signal |
| Inquiry asking about price first | Negative | Likely misaligned |
