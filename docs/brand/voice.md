# Oficio Taller — Brand Voice Reference
**Version:** 1.0 — March 2026
**For:** Diego (copywriter), Valentina (CMO review), all Materia Pod agents

---

## What This Document Is

This is not a style guide in the conventional marketing sense. It is a listening document — the same way the studio listens to territory before proposing architecture, this document listens to Oficio Taller before writing a single word on their behalf.

Every rule here traces back to something the studio actually does, says, or believes. Nothing is invented to make the brand sound better. If the writing doesn't emerge from the architecture and the people who make it, it isn't Oficio Taller.

## The Studio in Their Own Words

From their YouTube channel description — the most unguarded version of how they describe themselves:

> *"Somos un taller de arquitectura en constante búsqueda y aprendizaje, para convertir nuestro trabajo en experiencias y sensaciones inspiradas en cómo vivimos, invadimos y nos apropiamos de los espacios. Creemos en las tradiciones del oficio y el valor de la mano de obra, somos una especie de laboratorio que trabaja con las posibilidades de los materiales para generar atmósferas genuinas adaptadas a los medios y lenguajes actuales que reflejen la esencia de lo local."*

Read this slowly. Notice what they choose:

- **"constante búsqueda y aprendizaje"** — constant search and learning. Not expertise. Not mastery. Searching.
- **"experiencias y sensaciones"** — experiences and sensations. Not "spaces" or "architecture."
- **"invadimos y nos apropiamos"** — we invade and we appropriate. These are bold verbs. Architecture as a kind of claiming.
- **"tradiciones del oficio"** — traditions of craft. The word *oficio* is in the studio's name for a reason.
- **"el valor de la mano de obra"** — the value of labor. Not just craft as concept — the physical human labor of building.
- **"atmósferas genuinas"** — genuine atmospheres. Not designed atmospheres. *Genuine.*
- **"la esencia de lo local"** — the essence of the local. Not "inspired by local culture." The essence of it.

This paragraph is the voice. Everything that follows is how to use it.

## Tone

**Quiet.** The writing should not compete with the architecture for attention. It should open a door, not perform an entrance.

**Sensory and precise.** Abstract claims ("beautiful," "unique," "inspiring") cost nothing and mean nothing. Specific sensory details ("the sound of waves entering the space," "concrete that holds the heat of the afternoon") are irreplaceable.

**Present tense when possible.** The house exists now. The light falls now. Write it that way — it makes the reader feel present.

**Never promotional.** No exclamation marks. No calls to react, share, or follow. The work speaks; the caption accompanies.

**Unhurried.** Long sentences slow the reader down. Short sentences stop them. Use both deliberately. Never rush toward a point.

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Sensory before visual
Architecture photography shows the visual. The writing must carry what the photograph cannot: temperature, smell, sound, weight, the grain of a surface underfoot. If the caption could be written without having experienced the space, rewrite it.

**Wrong:** "A stunning home nestled into the dunes of Baja California Sur."
**Right:** "The dune enters the house. There is no clear moment where outside becomes inside — only a gradual shift in light and the smell of salt and dry grass."

### 2. Place before project
Every project belongs to a specific territory. Name it. Describe it. The territory is not background — it is a co-author.

**Wrong:** "A project in Baja California Sur, 2022."
**Right:** "Cerritos in the dry season: a shore wind, white dust, the blue gone flat by noon. Casa Atlas was built from that specific hour."

### 3. Inhabitation before objecthood
The studio's YouTube series is called *Habitar* — to dwell, to inhabit. This is intentional. The work is not a building to be admired from outside. It is a space to be lived inside. Show that.

**Wrong:** "A masterfully designed residence by Oficio Taller."
**Right:** "You wake up to the sound of surf and the ceiling already knows the light of 7am. Casa Surfhouse was designed around that particular hour."

### 4. Labor and craft are named, not implied
The studio explicitly values *la mano de obra* — the hand of the maker. Name the craftspeople, the materials, the techniques when the architect's intake includes them. This is not detail for detail's sake — it is respect.

**Wrong:** "Carefully crafted interiors."
**Right:** "The concrete was mixed and poured by local workers from Pescadero. The imperfections in the surface are intentional — they record the making."

### 5. Restraint over explanation
Do not explain what the photograph already shows. Do not over-caption. The reader can see the shadow; you don't need to write "a dramatic shadow falls across the wall." Trust the image. Add what the image cannot hold.

## Project Naming Conventions

Oficio Taller's project names are not marketing names — they are acts of naming that carry meaning. Use them with full intention.

| Project | Name origin | How to use it |
|---|---|---|
| **La Cueva** | The cave — literal geology of the site | "La Cueva" — always with the article, never abbreviated |
| **La Cáscara** | The shell or husk — the building as a thin enclosure | The name implies lightness and protection simultaneously |
| **Casa Atlas** | Named for the project — the dune as load-bearing | "Habitar la duna" is the studio's own subtitle — use it |
| **Casa Oasis** | The oasis — water in dryness, shelter in exposure | Implies relief and discovery, not luxury |
| **Jardín Mar** | Sea garden — the edge between two territories | Both words carry equal weight; do not reduce to one |
| **Horizonte** | Horizon — the meeting of land and sky | Always refers to orientation and outlook, not just view |
| **Surfhouse** | Named for its relationship to surf culture | Functional and direct — the name is not poetic |
| **Lomas** | Hills — the topography determines the project | Plural, geographic, grounded |
| **Albedo** | The reflectivity of a surface — a physics term | The most conceptually precise project name; handle carefully |
| **Pomona** | Named for the Roman goddess of fruit and abundance | Implies fertility, garden, cultivation |

**Rule:** Always use the project's proper name. Never describe a project generically when its name carries the meaning you need.

These appear in the studio's own descriptions and fit the voice naturally:

**English:**
inhabit · territory · atmosphere · threshold · materiality · craft · labor · the local · sensation · belonging · continuity · landscape · climate · vegetation · topography · light · shadow · texture · silence · ritual · place

### Words that do not belong

These are borrowed from luxury real estate and generic architecture marketing. None of them are permitted in any content:

**Never use (Spanish):**
lujoso · exclusivo · premium · icónico · impresionante · espectacular · sorprendente · orgullosos de presentar · de clase mundial · único en su tipo

**Never use (English):**
luxury · exclusive · premium · iconic · stunning · spectacular · amazing · proud to present · world-class · one-of-a-kind · dream home · breathtaking · elevated · curated lifestyle

**Use with extreme caution:**
beautiful · beautiful is not forbidden, but it should only appear when you cannot find the specific sensory detail that would make the reader feel the beauty themselves. "Beautiful" means you gave up.

## Platform-Specific Voice

### Instagram

**Format:**
- Spanish caption first, full thought
- Single blank line
- En dash: —
- English caption below, equivalent in meaning (not word-for-word translation)
- One blank line
- Hashtags on final line, never in the body of the caption

**Length:** 3–6 lines per language. Shorter is almost always better.

**Register:** The most intimate platform. Write as if someone is looking at the image quietly, not scrolling. One image, one feeling, one thought.

**Opening lines:** The first line must work without the rest. 8 words or fewer. No greeting, no introduction, no "We are excited to share." Begin mid-thought, mid-sensation, mid-place.

**Examples of strong Instagram openers:**
- *"La duna entra a la casa."* (The dune enters the house.)
- *"Concreto que guarda el calor de la tarde."* (Concrete that holds the afternoon heat.)
- *"El umbral no existe. Solo hay un cambio en la luz."* (The threshold does not exist. There is only a change in the light.)
- *"Aquí, la vegetación decide la planta."* (Here, the vegetation decides the floor plan.)

**Hashtag strategy:** 8–12 tags. Mix Spanish and English. Anchor to place and material, not to generic architecture aggregators.

Preferred tags: `#oficiotaller` `#arquitecturamexicana` `#habitarelmundo` `#arquitecturaresidencial` `#diseñomexicano` `#bajacaliforniasur` `#arquitetura` `#materialidad` `#mexicanarchitecture` `#architectureandnature` `#residentialarchitecture` `#interiordesign`

Avoid: `#luxuryhomes` `#dreamhome` `#homedesign` `#inspo` `#goals`

### YouTube

**Title format:**
`Oficio Taller | [Project Name] | [Sensory subtitle in Spanish]`

The subtitle is the most important part. It should not describe what the film shows — it should name what it feels like to inhabit the project. The studio's own examples:

- *Casa Atlas | Habitar la duna* — to inhabit the dune
- *La Cueva* — no subtitle needed; the name carries everything

**Strong subtitle patterns:**
- An infinitive verb: *Habitar la duna* / *Escuchar el mar* / *Pertenecer al cerro*
- A material fact: *Concreto y luz* / *La piedra y el viento*
- A sensory observation: *El sonido del agua al amanecer*

**Description format:**
Full Spanish description (3–5 sentences, project + territory + sensory thesis)
Blank line
—
Full English description (equivalent meaning, not literal translation)
Blank line
Project credits (architects, collaborators, location, year)
Blank line
#oficiotaller #arquitecturamexicana [additional tags]

**Description tone:** More expansive than Instagram. The viewer has already committed 2–4 minutes — they can hold a longer thought. But still: sensory, place-first, unhurried.

### Website (Cargo project pages)

**Structure:**
1. Project name + location + year (header, no styling needed — Cargo handles it)
2. Opening statement in Spanish (2–3 sentences, the sensory thesis of the project)
3. En dash separator
4. Opening statement in English (same thesis, different phrasing)
5. Body text alternating between languages or parallel columns (match Cargo's layout)
6. Credits: architects, collaborators, builders, photographers

**Tone:** The most detailed and the most grounded. The website is where a serious client will spend time. Write with depth. Include the site — its climate, its ecology, its existing conditions before the project arrived. Include the materials and why they were chosen. Include the collaboration.

**Length:** As long as the project deserves. A major project warrants 400–600 words. A smaller one, 200. Never pad. Never cut depth for brevity.

## Bilingual Writing Protocol

Diego writes Spanish first, always. This is not just a production rule — Spanish is the primary language of the studio, the region, and most of the craftspeople and builders involved in the work. The English follows from the Spanish, not the other way around.

**Translating with precision:**
- Translate meaning, not words
- Some Spanish terms have no direct English equivalent — in those cases, use the Spanish word in the English text and let it stand
- *Habitar* is one of these words. "To inhabit" is close but not exact. Use it, but acknowledge the gap when it matters
- *Oficio* means craft, trade, vocation, calling — all at once. "Craft" is the closest single word in English

**Do not:**
- Make the English version more formal than the Spanish
- Add words in English that weren't in the Spanish to "explain" something
- Use British English conventions (the studio operates in a Mexican/US context)

## What Valentina Checks Before Any Content Reaches the CEO

This is the brand review protocol, not a creative checklist. It is a test of honesty:

1. **Is every sensory detail traceable to the architect's intake notes?** If Diego invented an atmosphere that the architects didn't describe, the content is wrong — not because it isn't well-written, but because it isn't true.

2. **Does any word from the forbidden vocabulary appear?** One "stunning" fails the entire piece.

3. **Is the project name used correctly?** Generic description in place of the proper name is always wrong.

4. **Does the English feel as considered as the Spanish?** If the English reads like a translation, rewrite it. It should read like it was written natively.

5. **Does the content communicate inhabitation or objecthood?** If it photographs the building from the outside without bringing the reader inside, revise toward inhabitation.

6. **Is there any exaggeration?** The studio's work is strong. It does not need inflation. Understatement is always preferable to overstatement.
