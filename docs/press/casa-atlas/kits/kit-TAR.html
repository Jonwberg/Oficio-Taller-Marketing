The house positions itself behind the dune and accepts its topographic condition as the primary architectural argument. The dune is not an obstacle resolved; it is the principle that organizes plan, section, and program.

The answer was sectional — a series of level changes that ensures the horizon remains visible from every space in the house, including those set furthest from the shore. The sea is not a privilege reserved for the primary facades; it is a condition of habitation throughout. I think this is an architectural problem worth publishing.

The house was designed for a chef and his family, and functions as a node connecting local producers, fishermen, and farmers from the region. Architecture as infrastructure for a practice of living rooted in territory.

Project text

Atlas begins with a premise: the coastal dune is not backdrop but ground and shelter.

The house is positioned behind it, accepting the dune's topographic condition as an architectural argument — not an obstacle to resolve, but an organizing principle for plan, section, and program. The dune provides natural shelter from prevailing winds and becomes the first move: volumes step with its geometry, patios open between them to frame the landscape without capturing it.

The site's challenge was depth. Not every space could have a direct relationship with the ocean frontage. The answer was sectional. A series of level changes ensures the horizon is visible from every space in the house — including those set back from the primary facade. The sea is not a privilege reserved for the front rooms; it is a condition of habitation throughout. This was not a view problem. It was a sectional argument.

Volumes separate to produce intermediate patios: thresholds that belong neither fully to interior nor exterior. This strategy is not formal — it is climatic. The patios manage wind, shadow, and light in a territory where midday flattens everything and evening transforms it. They are the climate system of the house.

The materials — concrete, local wood, clay — are not finishes: they are the site in other forms. They anchor the house to the ground and age alongside it. Light is the transforming agent: morning activates the entrance facade and the edible garden, afternoon penetrates the bedrooms, night turns the terraces into instruments of sky observation.

The house was designed for a chef and his family. Two kitchens are the heart of the program — the upper opens to the terrace, pool, and Pacific; the lower connects to an edible garden and outdoor dining oriented toward the dune. The architecture follows the life that was going to happen in it. It also functions as a node in a network of local producers, fishermen, and farmers: a space open to the exchange of knowledge, produce, and table. Architecture as infrastructure for a practice of inhabiting territory.
