Editorial proposal — Casa Atlas — Architecture, Mexican craft, and the inhabited dune — Cerritos, BCS, 2025

Dear Domus editorial team,

I would like to propose Casa Atlas for your consideration — a private residence completed in 2025 in Cerritos, Baja California Sur, Mexico, designed by Oficio Taller in collaboration with Estudio Homística on interiors.

The project sits behind a coastal dune and accepts its topographic condition as an architectural argument. But what I believe may interest Domus most is how the house was built with — and for — a culture of making. The interiors are a conversation with Mexican artisans: hand-thrown clay vessels, local wood furniture with irregular grain that openly celebrates imperfection, stone lamps by Mexican designers, walls dressed in textiles woven by artisan women from the region. The kitchen — there are two — is the center of the program. The house was designed for a chef whose practice connects producers, fishermen, and farmers from the territory.

The architecture and the objects inside it are part of the same argument: that building well in this part of Mexico means building from it — from the materials, the landscape, the people who inhabit it.

Project text

Casa Atlas was designed to hold what its inhabitants bring to it: the salt, the fire, the bread.

The house sits behind a coastal dune in Cerritos, Baja California Sur. The dune is not scenography — it is structure, shelter, and the first architectural move. Volumes step with its topography, patios open between them to frame the landscape, and a sectional strategy ensures the ocean horizon is visible from every space in the house, including those furthest from the shore.

The interiors, developed with Estudio Homística, are a sustained conversation with Mexican material culture. Hand-thrown clay vessels from Oaxacan potters, local wood furniture whose irregular grain openly celebrates the beauty of imperfection, stone lamps carved by Mexican designers, walls dressed in textiles woven by artisan women from the Sierra. Every object has a place of origin and a story. The accumulation is not decorative — it is an argument about how a house in this territory should be furnished.

The program was conceived for a chef and his family. Two kitchens are at the heart of it: the upper opens entirely to the terrace, the pool, and the Pacific — you cook facing the ocean. The lower connects to an edible garden and an outdoor dining space oriented toward the dune. The house also functions as a node in a local network of producers, fishermen, and farmers from the region. Tradition and hospitality are structural conditions, not gestures.

The materials throughout — concrete, local wood, clay, stone — are calibrated to age with the territory. They are the site in other forms. Light moves through the house across the day: morning activates the entrance and the garden, afternoon settles into the rooms, evening turns the terraces into an observatory of open sky. The concrete holds the warmth of the day and releases it after sunset.
